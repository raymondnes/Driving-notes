# Driving instruction and learning

This learning material is designed for you who might be new to motoring. Not only does a driving license give you freedom to move around, but it also brings a lot of responsibility. No one is alone in traffic. A responsible driver drives safely without risking others, is good at observing their surroundings, knows the rules and regulations and obeys them.

Getting a driving licence is to some the only goal of driving instruction. You may feel that after taking the driving test successfully you are a good driver. However, you will be involved with road traffic throughout your life. You will encounter both new types of situations and drivers as well as learn something new every day.
Bear in mind that a responsible driver will continuously develop their skills. They observe the changes in legislation and practise their driving skills in traffic.

The purpose of this material is to help you reach your goal of becoming a responsible driver! It will introduce to you the traffic rules and regulations, situations on the road, driving instruction itself, the driving test, and it will enhance your learning.

A responsible driver is the aim!
Thus, the main objective of a driving school is to mould you into a responsible driver who can drive safely, socially and ecologically. You also have to be able to estimate your own actions in traffic, as well as its consequences.

Responsible

A responsible driver has a realistic idea of their driving skills and wishes to develop as a driver. To cope in traffic, you will have to learn such competence and skills that you are able to behave independently, safely taking others into account, and act as ecologically as possible while driving!

Safe

A safe driver can control the direction, location and speed of their vehicle. They knows the traffic rules and regulations and can estimate their own and other road users? actions and the potential risks thereof. A safe driver can anticipate these risks and for example lower their own speed. Anticipating and avoiding risky situations are the top responsibilities of a driver!

Social

A social driver respects the rights of other road users, can anticipate the actions of others and behaves predictably. A social driver can control their own emotions. They recognize the various road user groups and their special needs (eg children, the elderly and the handicapped).

# The car and how to handle it

To be able to drive a car smoothly, safely and economically, you have to know the structure of a car and its functional principles. A driving school won't make you a skilled mechanic, but you should know and understand some general concepts.

Controlling the car and handling it mechanically involve both your body and mind. You use your limbs when you shift gears, depress the pedals or turn the steering wheel. Just think, before starting to drive when was the last time you used all your limbs to do different things, at the same time monitoring the changes in traffic in front of you? Handling the car mechanically is, however, only a small part of driving. First and foremost you drive a car using your eyes. Not even a skillful driver can cope in traffic without good perception.

# A car's behaviour and safety features

Adjusting proper speed is characteristic of a responsible and safe driver. You should adjust your speed according to the driving conditions at all times. With your own speed you control your own as well as the other drivers' risk level.

In addition, the driver needs to know how a car behaves and which forces influence it when driving. When it comes to the mechanics of a vehicle, the most important ones are Newton?s laws of motion.

Motion mechanics of a car
All bodies are affected by certain physical forces. In terms of driving, the most important of the concepts in Newtonian physics is kinetic energy.

Kinetic energy and impact forces
An object continues to move along a straight line path without turning or changing its speed or stays at rest unless an external force acts upon it. In other words, when a car weighing more than 1,000 kg is set to motion, it has gained kinetic energy and it tends to continue straight forward. The amount of kinetic energy depends on the mass of the car (its weight, load and passengers) and its speed. The kinetic energy of a car in motion is the mass multiplied by the square of speed. If speed is doubled, equivalent kinetic energy is quadrupled (furthermore, if speed is tripled, kinetic energy is nine times as much, and so on).

Kinetic energy and impact forces
Speed is the decisive factor in the amount of kinetic energy (the square of speed influences in many different ways, for example, if speed doubles, the stopping distance quadruples). Correspondingly, if impact speed is doubled, impact energy is quadrupled. This is why speed before a collision has a great influence in a crash - at higher speeds the outcome is a lot worse. If a car collides with a stationary object or there is a head-on collision of two cars both driving at a speed of 50 km/h, the impact force caused by and applied on a person weighing 80 kg is 3,000 kg. If the impact involves a speed of 100 km/h, impact force is 12,000 kg. This is definitely a fatal collision.

Centrifugal force
A young driver typically exits a curve at too high a speed, losing control of the vehicle. Why doesn't a car navigate around a curve? Let's take a look at a hammer thrower. The thrower swings the hammer in a wide circle and lets go of it. When loose, the hammer goes straight ahead, away from the thrower. This is what a car also does. When you lose traction of the tyres, steering no longer follows your turning of the wheel and the car carries on straight forward. Likewise, the load on top of the car can come loose and will move straight forward, that is, the momentum is forward according to the Newton's laws of motion.

oss of traction, friction force
Controlling a car is based on traction between the tyres and the road. A tyre touches the surface of the road only about the width of a hand and your possibility to accelerate, steer and brake depends on friction. Likewise, the speed of rounding a curve depends on friction. When the car is stationary, there is the maximum amount of friction but when you move off, friction diminishes. Proper speed depends on the surface of the road and condition of your tyres (there is most friction when the condition of your summer tyres is good and you are driving on dry asphalt).
Loss of Traction
A tyre's grip is determined by the friction between the tyre and the road surface.

Under normal conditions, there is grip in both the driving direction and sideways.
When traction is lost in the driving direction, it is simultaneously lost laterally. In this case, the vehicle can no longer be steered.

Stopping distance
Kinetic energy is transformed into thermal energy when braking. The stopping distance is therefore dependent on the condition of the tyres and the road surface. The stopping distance consists of the braking distance and the reaction distance, ie the reaction time of the driver and the distance the vehicle travels after that.
Let's see what happens: a driver spots acute danger, eg a child running to a pedestrian crossing. The driver applies the brakes only after one to two seconds after detecting danger. Why not right away? The reason is the reaction time. This time varies from one person to another but a healthy, well-rested person reacts in one to two seconds. If a person is ill or tired, the reaction time is longer. A responsible and experienced driver reacts in about one second and applies the brakes after perceiving danger. Depending on speed, the car travels a certain distance. This distance grows in relation to speed: the car travels twice the distance if speed is doubled. The braking distance begins when the brakes are fully applied and ends when the car comes to a complete stop. The braking distance is influenced by speed and grows in the same way as kinetic energy, with the square of speed.

Stopping distance of emergency braking in various conditions
The stopping distance consists of two factors: the reaction distance and the braking distance. The decisive factor is, however, the driving conditions. Water and slippery road surface significantly increase the braking distance. The distance is doubled on wet asphalt when compared to dry surface. At all times, take the weather and road conditions into consideration.

Pay special attention when you drive on an unpaved road.

Rounding a curve and driving uphill and downhill
Centrifugal force tries to push a car outward when rounding a corner. The side force of a tyre, as well as friction keep a car on the road as long as speed isn't too high. Even a slight increase in speed may lead to a run-off-road collision. The frictional forces are entirely used to keep the side traction, leaving no traction for the driving direction in the worst case. So every curve requires its own speed. This speed depends on how sharp the curve is and on traction. A curve you've driven through a hundred times in summer can be four times more slippery in winter. A responsible driver anticipates this and slows down early enough in bad driving conditions. They are also prepared for a potential obstacle in an unseen part of a curve. Always drive into a curve so slowly that you are able to stop the car without losing control of steering. If you lose the control of a car in a curve, the correct way out of the situation is to depress the clutch right away. Do not brake! It's only a freely rotating wheel that has traction on the road. When driving downhill, remember that your speed can easily accelerate too high. Driving uphill is a challenge for the engine and as a result the cooling system may fail.

Road surface, the environment and conditions
A slippery road surface may surprise a driver even in summer. In addition to ice and snow, other causes of a slippery surface are:

oil on road surface
water on recently applied asphalt
wet leaves in autumn
Never hit a pothole too fast. Not only will it damage your tyres but it will also cause the loss of control of your car. Slow down before a pothole.

Certain environments also require a lower speed. The permitted speed limit in a residential precinct is 20 km/h. When in a pedestrian zone, the speed is even lower - 15 km/h. In addition, in case of road construction, speed may be restricted to secure the safety of the workers. Take them into consideration by slowing down even if there is no sign to tell you so. A responsible driver also makes an effort to slow down in the vicinity of nursery schools, hospitals, schools and playgrounds to a speed lower than indicated in the speed limit sign. Children are unpredictable in traffic. Enhance your perception especially when a school bus stops to let the children out.

Road work
When there is road work, always slow down to secure the workers' safety. When a car and a person collide, a person is always the weaker party!

Car safety
When talking about car safety, it is often divided into active and passive safety. A car is at its safest when it is balanced to drive in different weather conditions, traffic environments, and while loaded with weight and cargo within the allowed limits.

Active safety
By active safety we refer to safety systems and improvements in a car, which are designed to avoid accidents in advance. Cars today are designed to make driving as easy as possible. The easier the controls and the steering are, the more a driver can focus on monitoring traffic. Also visibility plays an important role. A driver has to see outside and other road users have to see the car (ie the windscreen and its devices, other windows, mirrors, headlamps and reflectors). Technical features and driver assistance systems (tyres, suspension and brakes) also influence maneuverability.

Technical features and driver assistance systems (tyres, suspension and brakes) also influence maneuverability.

ASR

ASR (Anti-slip Regulation or Traction control system) is a system designed to prevent loss of traction of driven wheels when moving off or accelerating. When the system detects loss of traction on a slippery surface, it applies the brakes of a driven wheel and reduces engine power.

ESC

ESC (Electronic Stability Control or Electronic Stability Program) is a vehicle stability system which intervenes when it detects a probable loss of steering control. Typical causes of it are too high a speed in a particular situation, over- or under steer or loss of control. The system tends to regain control by applying the brakes to wheels individually and/or by regulating the engine power or by operating the transmission. If for example the rear end of a car swerves to the right in a left curve, the system applies the brake to the right front wheel to regain control.

ABS and BAS

ABS (Anti-lock Braking System) prevents the brakes from locking up while braking. Only a wheel that is rotating freely maintains tractive contact with the road surface, so a vehicle equipped with ABS can still be steered despite heavy braking. Maintaining the control of a car is vital even though the braking distance might become a little longer. The brake pedal should be depressed with a minimum pressure force of 50 kg when braking hard. Only this will ensure efficient braking. A car may also be equipped with BAS (Brake Assist System) - a system which maximizes braking force even if the brakes are not fully applied. The system detects the need for more force, overrides and fully applies the brakes.

Passive safety
By passive safety we refer to minimizing a driver's and passengers' injuries after an accident has already occurred. With the following features passive safety is the best possible:

impact absorbing body structure to protect in head-on, side impact, rear-end, run-off-road and rollover collisions
high-strength body with a deformable impact region for vehicle occupants
secured door locks and seat fastening as well as windscreen durability in case an airbag is inflated.
equipment such as seatbelts, children's safety devices, head- and neck rests and various airbags.
The passengers' safety devices don't, however, matter if for example the seatbelts are not fastened.

Passive safety
Part of passive safety is the risk posed to a pedestrian in an accident. Some newer models are fitted with airbags which are inflated in an impact to protect a pedestrian.

The driver needs to bear in mind that no safety systems, however good they may be, will help unless the driver is alert. The most important safety feature in a car is the driver!

Pedestrian airbag
The pedestrian airbag technology helps to protect pedestrians in certain situations when struck by the vehicles front-end with a consequent impact to the hood and the area around the windscreen wiper recess and A-pillar, where there may be a risk of head impacts.

# Owning a car and looking after it
The owner, the holder and the driver are responsible for the vehicle. The authorities monitor the condition the vehicles are in by conducting spot checks in the streets and on the roads. A vehicle which is not in good condition and serviced may pose danger and is also very uneconomical to drive.

The major factors when buying a car of your own are safety, economy and defining what you will use the car for. It's often the case that a person chooses an affordable, often an older model of a car as his or her first car. The logic behind it is that it doesn't pay to buy a better car because a new driver may end up having a collision. This is, however, wrong because you compromise on safety and economy. Compromising on safety is always a poor investment.

Economy
There is a variety of cars. A car which has an engine with the engine displacement of more than 2000cc consumes many times more fuel than an engine which has a lower displacement. Another factor which influences fuel efficiency is whether the car is equipped with a petrol or diesel engine. To figure out what kind of car would be most fuel efficient you need to consider how many kilometres you will be driving annually. If you transport goods on a regular basis or tow a trailer, it makes more sense to buy a car with a bigger engine. If, on the other hand, the main need for a car is to travel short distances to work and back and usually alone, you might want to consider a fuel efficient city car. So it makes a difference what your choice of a car is. At the end of the day, no matter how fuel efficient your car is, the most important thing is you as a driver.

Buying a car is always a big deal. So get ready for it and carefully consider the issues connected with choosing a car. You either buy a car from a private person or from a car dealer. A car owner has several legally mandated responsibilities, which accrue a certain amount of expenses. For example, an owner must register and insure their new car within seven days of the purchase.

When you buy a car
A car dealer is happy to assist you with the choice of a car. Some have applications to help you analyze your needs. A car dealer always gives you a three-month warranty on the car that you buy at a dealer's. You can also find hundreds of sites on the internet where private people advertise cars for sale. The warranty for these cars is the so-called "rear-light warranty": once the rear lights disappear from the sight of the seller, your warranty is no longer valid.

Buying a car from a private seller
If you buy a car from a private person, ask a more experienced friend or family member to come along. Ask if you could do a little test drive - you may be able to spot if there are major problems with the car. The best way is to have a condition survey made either at a garage or a vehicle inspection station. They don't do it for free but to avoid a false move it's definitely worth having done.

Registering a vehicle
A vehicle used in traffic must be, according to the law, registered and the Finnish Vehicle Administration must be notified of possible conversions. The registration obligation concerns the owner of an engine-powered vehicle or a trailer attached to it or the vehicle attached to a trailer (The owner is responsible for the vehicle s/he owns as well as notifying about the changes in its registration) In addition, the registration obligation also concerns the holder. By a holder we refer to the person who mainly uses the car but doesn't own it. The owner, the holder and the driver are responsible for the roadworthiness of a vehicle and its inspection.

lectronic registration
Today, a vehicle can also be registered electronically. Thanks to the digital certificate, the security code that replaces the vehicle registration certificate, you no longer need to present any documents when registering a change of ownership. In practice, the seller applies for a digital certificate for the vehicle in Traficom's online service and hands over the digital certificate to the buyer. The buyer then uses the digital certificate to register the vehicle. The change of ownership of a vehicle must be reported to the register within seven days of the transfer of ownership. If the owner of the vehicle so wishes, they can check the up-to-date technical information of their own vehicle from Traficom's electronic services.

Vehicle tax
Vehicle tax consists of a basic tax component and a tax on motive force charged for diesel vehicles. A tax on motive force is charged for vehicles using other force or other fuels than petrol. The owner of the car is obliged to pay vehicle tax as stated by the government. The tax can be paid in one or three parts. The amount of the tax is determined according to emissions, ie the more a car pollutes, the higher the tax. In addition to the insurance fee, the amount of the tax and the fees for inspection, you also have to add to the total sum the amount of money spent on petrol and maintenance. It all boils down to the fact that owning a car is not very cheap.

Car tax and emissions
In 2008, the car tax was tied to carbon dioxide emissions (CO²), which are directly proportional to fuel emissions. The aim was to encourage car buyers to prefer new, low-emission cars. This objective was achieved, as emissions decreased by approximately 4.6% in 2008 when compared to the previous year. Before that, CO² emissions increased by nearly 15% between 1990 and 2007.

Motor insurance
Motor insurance is obligatory for all motor vehicles and must be taken out by the owner or the holder of the vehicle. Motor insurance is obligatory even when a vehicle is driven on a private road, in the courtyard, in the forest or on ice. When a vehicle is transferred to a new owner, new motor insurance has to be taken out by the new owner.

0-Day Off-Road Period
If you are not going to use your car for at least 30 consecutive days, it is advisable to notify your insurance company.
When the vehicle is off the road for 30 consecutive days, you may be entitled to a discount for the off-road period.

A no-claims discount and losing it
Your motor insurance fee may be lowered if no incidents have taken place. A no-claims discount applies for those years when a vehicle has been on road for at least 180 days and there have been no claims to the insurance company. The first no-claim year earns the owner a 10% discount and the following 5%. The maximum bonus allowed is 70%. Therefore, someone who has had several incidents may pay three times higher insurance fees than someone who has had no claims to the insurance company. In case of a minor incident after you have already earned some no-claim discount, it's worth considering whether to self-finance the loss in order to avoid an increased future premium - or to finance the loss by compensation from the insurance company.

Vehicle inspection
In addition to roadside spot checks made by the police, the roadworthiness of a vehicle is checked in annual inspections. To ensure safety for the user and others, the condition of a car and its equipment is inspected. Cars need to be inspected at the latest on the date of first registration as marked in the registration document. A passenger car and a van may be inspected four months before the due date at the earliest.

It's very easy to have your car inspected. You simply drive to a vehicle inspection station (you may want to make an appointment) and sign up for inspection. What you need to have is the technical section of your registration document.

reparing for inspection
Before the inspection it may be a good idea to have your car serviced or at least have the lights, the condition of the tyres and their air pressure, the cleanliness of the engine compartment and the function of ancillary controls (windscreen washer, heater etc) checked. Make sure that you have the warning triangle in the car. You may also want to take your car to a garage for emission measuring so that the readings can be adjusted to the right level.

Servicing your car
For your car to maintain its value and for it to remain safe to drive, the owner needs to make sure it is serviced regularly. Some maintenance can be done by the owner, but a majority should be done by a qualified mechanic. You should always make sure to conduct pre-drive checks on a vehicle before driving any distance with it.

Smoking in the car
If you are smoker, you should not engage in the habit inside the car. Besides the obvious health risks, there are several other reasons:

Tobacco smoke contains tar, which will gather on the windows and other surfaces over time. Dust will attach to this sticky layer and decrease visibility. It is very difficult to remove this residue afterwards.
The resale value of the vehicle will decrease. Cigarette smoke smell is very difficult to remove.
Passive smoking also poses health risks to the passengers.
Lighting a cigarette, holding it in your hand, and other unnecessary activities will always impede your ability to observe your surroundings, which is the most important part of driving.
Please remember that it is illegal to smoke in the car if you have a child under the age of 15 as a passenger.

Pre-drive check
Before you drive off, you should perform a pre-drive check of your vehicle. Perform this checklist in the same order every time and become accustomed to it so you don't miss anything. Check the following things:

1 General state 2 Fluids and oil 3 Lights 4 Other checks

1 - General state

Take a glance over your car and the surroundings. Check if there are any fluid leaks or if there have been any changes to the general state of your car, such as signs of tampering or vandalism.
Look at your tyres to make sure that there is plenty of air inside and they don't look loose. At the same time make sure to look underneath the car to check that there aren't any obstructions underneath, in front, or behind the car.

2 - Fluids and oil

Fuel, break fluids, oil. These checks under the bonnet should be done regularly.

Open the bonnet.
When checking the oil, lift the oil stick out, wipe it clean, then stick it back in before pulling it out again to check how high along the stick the oil level reaches.
Check the amount of wiper fluid. In some car models a light on the dashboard will also alert you when the wiper fluid is running low.
Check that the car battery is securely attached and the wires are firmly connected.
Check the amount of break fluids.
In order to check coolant fluid levels, it is easiest to check the coolant reservoir. You should never unscrew the top when the engine is warm. Due to pressure the hot coolant fluid can surge out of the reservoir.

3 - Lights

Turn on power in your car and switch on the parking lights and emergency lights. Check that the following lights also work:
headlamps,
high beams,
brake lights. For this you might want to reverse close to a wall so you can see them working from your mirrors.

4 - Other checks

Check the air pressure in your tyres regularly. The side of the tyre should in most cases have a marking from which you can see the recommended pressure. You can also check this from the service manual for your car.
Check that your headlamps break lights, and signals are clean so the light is not obstructed. Also make sure that your windows are clean and you can see outside properly.

Coolant is used for cooling an engine. Coolant is a mixture of water and antifreeze (glycol). Check before adding coolant what kind of mixture ratio is recommended by the manufacturer. Usually the mixture ratio is half and half. Never mix coolants of different colour.

Towing another vehicle
Towing a car is risky business. The driver in the car that's being towed, if inexperienced, can't steer the car properly. Because of the reaction time and distance towing always poses safety risks. For example when the engine isn't running, power steering won't work. Remember that you can always get professional help to tow your car for you. Your insurance company may not pay for damages incurred during towing if your car hasn't been professionally towed.

When the engine won't start
Usually, if the engine doesn't start, it isn't getting either fuel or power from the battery. If the battery is empty (such as if the lights have been left on by accident) then you may recharge it by connecting another battery to it. Remember to always place positive on positive first, then the negative leads after. The car's internal computer (ECM-system) can be very fragile to sudden bursts of electricity, such as the case when a a car is jumpstarted with another battery. Make sure you won't make a faulty connection. Also, make sure to check the service manual of your car to see if you can jumpstart your car in this way at all. When in doubt, it is always safer to call a professional or have your car towed to a qualified mechanic.

Towing a vehicle (Decree on the Use of Vehicles on the Road 14 §)
When a vehicle, which cannot or must not be driven by means of its own propulsion, is towed with a tow-rope or tow-bar the distance between the towed vehicle and the towing vehicle shall be at least 3 metres and not more than 6 metres. A clearly visible flag shall be attached to the middle part of the tow-rope or the tow-bar. A vehicle with the brakes out of order may be towed only with a bar, a boom or a dolly and a vehicle with the steering out of order may be towed only when lifted to rest upon a boom or with a dolly.
Simultaneous towing of two vehicles with the same towing vehicle is prohibited. Towed vehicle must not be heavier than the towing vehicle without a compelling reason. The driver of a towed vehicle shall be a holder of a driving licence authorizing him to drive such a vehicle

Servicing a car
The owner must take care of having the car serviced as stated by the manufacturer. Servicing takes place after driving a certain number of kilometres (eg 15.000 km) How often this needs to be done is written in the car manual. Newer cars have an indicator in the dashboard informing the driver about the need to be serviced. The condition of a car is checked when it's being serviced. Worn and dirty parts and liquids - such as filters, oils and spark plugs - are changed. For the warranty not to expire, new cars must be serviced at the right time.

Seasonal servicing
If you don't drive much, a season service is worth considering. It's done in autumn before the arrival of the winter and in spring after the winter.

Spring service

When doing spring service, change the tyres. Also change the oil because when you start the car in cold weather, fuel mixes with the engine oil and weakens its lubrication efficiency. Besides, looking after the cleanliness is not at all a bad thing. Clean your car regularly from outside and inside. Waxing helps to keep the paint more beautiful. A layer of wax also repels some of the dirt and dust.

Autumn service

The purpose of autumn service is to prepare your car for the winter. A skillful driver changes the winter tyres, adds antifreeze and checks the condition of the battery.

Problem waste
Many liquids used in a car are problem waste. Under no circumstances must they be burned or dumped into sewage, the environment or waterway. Such liquids are:

used engine oil
coolant, brake fluid and clutch fluid Also car batteries are problem waste. Take the fluids and batteries to a local problem waste recycling centre.

Refueling the car
Before, refueling cars was relatively simple, you just had to choose whether to fill up with petrol or diesel. Today there are more options.

Electric

Charging electric cars is easy.

Grab your car's charging cable. In Europe, the most commonly used connector is Type-2 (Mennekes). With the Type-2 connector, you can charge an electric car with a power of up to 22kW. There are practically three different ways to charge electric cars: Mode 2 (charging with a portable charger from, for example, a home socket), Mode 3 (charging from a fixed charging station) and Mode 4 (quick charging).
Connect the other end of the charging cable to the charging station
Connect the other end of the charging cable to the charging port of your car
Charging stations with different capacities charge the car at different speeds. You should check the charging speed in the car's user manual.

Gas

Refueling a gas car works as follows:

First make the payment to the payment terminal.
Remove the refueling connector from its holder.
Connect the refueling connector to the car's filling valve, lock the connector in place.
Press the green button to start refueling.
When refueling is complete, the light next to the price display will turn green.
Unlock the connector and remove the refueling connector from the filling opening. NOTE! If necessary, lift the collar of the upper part of the refueling connector upwards so that the lock opens better
Put the refueling connector back in place and lock its fastening.

Combustion engine

When refueling a car with a combustion engine, proceed as follows:

Make sure what fuel you fill your vehicle with. Gasoline-powered cars have different octane requirements, i.e. the car can be filled with 95-octane or 98-octane. Check the user manual to find out which quality you can fill your car with.
Make the payment to the payment terminal.
Lift the desired fuel refueling gun from its holder and insert it into the car's refueling opening
Gently press the trigger of the refueling gun down
Continue refueling until the refueling gun clicks. This is a sign that the tank is getting full.
Release the refueling gun and push it back into its holder.
